Introduction

Our brains appear primed to process faces, reflecting the central role faces play in social life by conveying identity, intent, and affect.
Emotional expressions are particularly consequential as they enable observers to infer others' internal states and co-ordinate behaviour in real time.
Despite an extensive literature on emotion perception, the neural systems that support the recognition and interpretation of facial emotions remain unclear, which is becoming increasingly relevant as social interaction increasingly occurs through digital platforms populated by avatars and virtual faces.

The use of avatars, (computer-generated human representations) is increasing across social media, gaming, virtual reality, and augmented reality.
As interaction with avatars increases, the ability of virtual faces to convey emotion convincingly becomes essential for social acceptance and effective interaction.
Real faces express emotion through subtle muscle actions; virtual faces rely on pre-programmed or algorithmic expressions that vary in realism, dynamicity, and perceived authenticity.
These differences raise key questions: do virtual expressions engage the same neural mechanisms as real faces?
Do different emotions produce distinct neural responses?
And finally, is processing emotional expressions in virtual faces fundamentally different from processing emotional expressions in real faces?
We address these questions using functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) to compare how the brain processes emotional expressions in real and virtual faces.

Affective neuroscience work has identified a set of basic emotions, happiness, sadness, anger, fear, disgust, and surprise, with neutral faces serving as a baseline, and introduced the Facial Action Coding System (FACS) to catalogue the facial muscle actions that produce visible expressions.
Cross-cultural research has long supported the biological roots and broad universality of these expressions, even preliterate communities reliably recognized and produced the same basic expressions observed in Western populations.
Yet cultural differences still modulate how emotions are produced and represented mentally, where the core muscular patterns are conserved but expressive nuance and interpretation vary across groups.

Constructionist theories argue that emotions are constructed experiences resulting from the brain's interpretation of internal and external stimuli.
Behavioral findings support this view as contextual cues routinely bias emotion perception, altering valence and categorical judgments even for prototypical expressions.
Neutral faces can be perceived as fearful or happy depending on preceding context, and using emotion labels can shape how facial cues are integrated before an explicit categorization occurs.
These contextual effects are often automatic and difficult to suppress, highlighting the role of distributed, domain-general networks in emotion perception.

Multivariate analyses sometimes recover emotion-predictive patterns both locally and across distributed networks, but findings are often inconsistent.
fMRI implicates a broad network, including medial PFC, amygdala, fusiform gyrus, superior temporal sulcus, and insula in emotion perception, with certain emotions preferentially engaging specific areas.
fNIRS studies have likewise reported variable PFC responses to facial emotion, with some reporting increases, others decreases, or null effects in oxygenated hemoglobin (HbO) depending on task parameters and emotion type.
This underscores the need for targeted comparisons of how different emotions and kinds of faces are represented in the brain.

Avatars can be engineered using FACS-based principles and often convey many basic emotions accurately, yet some emotions (e.g., disgust) remain challenging to render.
In some instances virtual faces elicit different recognition patterns than real faces.
Neuroimaging shows that, although avatars are frequently processed like real faces, perceived authenticity and realism modulate neural responses.
For example, fearful human faces elicit stronger responses than fearful avatar faces in regions such as the superior temporal sulcus and insula, while EEG work has reported stronger theta and alpha responses to avatars under certain conditions.
Observers' responses are also influenced by the degree to which an avatar resembles their own appearance or habitual expressions.

The "uncanny valley" phenomenon, where near-real but imperfect virtual faces provoke unease, further complicates perception and neural responses.
Electrophysiological markers such as the N170 show non-linear sensitivity to realism.
Abstract or stylized faces can generate different amplitudes than realistic human faces, indicating early perceptual differences in how the brain processes face realness.

Despite substantive EEG and fMRI work, fNIRS investigations comparing facial emotion in real and virtual faces are scarce.
Existing fNIRS studies with avatars have focused chiefly on bodily expressions rather than facial emotion, and the only dynamic-avatar facial study used fMRI and examined only fear versus neutral.
To address this gap, we use fNIRS to compare cortical activation and functional connectivity elicited by static real and virtual faces across the full set of basic emotions, enabling a controlled comparison of face type and emotional content.

fNIRS is a portable, non-invasive technique that measures changes in oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin using near-infrared light and the Modified Beer-Lambert Law.
It offers greater ecological validity and tolerance for movement than MRI, while providing sufficient temporal resolution to capture hemodynamic responses associated with emotional processing.
fNIRS has restricted depth sensitivity, lower spatial resolution than fMRI, and susceptibility to hair and skin-related signal issues, but these limitations are mitigated through methodological refinements, such as high-density optode arrangements, short-separation channels, and motion correction techniques.

This work combines General Linear Model (GLM) analyses to quantify localized activation magnitudes with functional connectivity measures, specifically Wavelet Transform Coherence (WTC) to characterise time-frequency relationships between cortical regions.
The GLM models task events convolved with a canonical hemodynamic response to estimate region-specific responses across emotions and face types.
WTC captures non-stationary, frequency-specific coherence between channels, helping to distinguish genuine interregional coupling from systemic noise.

fNIRS is particularly sensitive to prefrontal processes central to emotion perception and regulation.
Few studies have examined the interaction of facial emotion and avatar realism with fNIRS, and to our knowledge no prior work has used a within-subject, fully crossed fNIRS design comparing all basic emotions across real and virtual faces.
We therefore hypothesise that (1) virtual and real faces will produce distinct activation and connectivity profiles, and (2) different emotional expressions will evoke unique neural signatures.
Given the exploratory nature of this study, we do not prespecify detailed directional predictions.
Instead, this study aims to map how emotion category and face realism jointly shape cortical activation and network dynamics, informing the design of emotionally expressive avatars for education, clinical, and human-computer interaction contexts.

Pilot

Participants
A total of 61 undergraduate students from Ontario Tech University participated in the pilot study as part of the SONA participant pool.
This sample consisted of 61 participants (20 males and 41 females, M = 20.84, SD = 3.25, range = 17 to 38).

Stimuli
One hundred and forty images of facial expressions from the racially diverse affective expression (RADIATE) and UIBVFED datasets were used.
The RADIATE contains perceptually validated images of racially and ethnically diverse participants, aged 18-30 years old, each expressing 16 different emotions.
The UIBVFED dataset contains a set of 20 virtual characters that are also ethnically diverse, aged 20-80 years old expressing 32 emotions.
The UIBVFED facial expressions were created using blendshapes, a tool that represents and manipulates clusters of facial landmarks similar to that of facial action units (AUs).
Adult models (5 males and 5 females) from each dataset were identified and matched between sets on face shape, sex, skin tone, and hair colour.
Images of each model expressing seven emotions (Anger, Disgust, Fear, Joy, Sadness, Surprise, Neutral) were selected.
Expressions were selected for each model that closely align with Ekman's six basic emotions plus neutral.
The intensity of the emotional expressions were matched as well across both datasets.

Design
The first stage of the task required participants to select which emotion was portrayed by an individual virtual avatar.
For each trial, they were shown a fixation cross for one second, then a photo of an avatar expressing an emotion was displayed in the center of the screen.
Participants were then asked to identify the emotion being expressed by selecting one of the seven emotions.
Only the virtual avatars from the UIBVFED stimulus set were used in this emotion recognition task, as the RADIATE dataset has already been validated for emotion recognition.

The second stage of the task required participants to rate the similarity of expressions displayed by virtual avatars compared to real human models.
Participants were given instructions to rate the similarity of the expressions displayed on the faces, not of the faces themselves.
Participants were shown a fixation cross for one second.
Following this, a pair of photos was selected randomly and displayed side-by-side on screen, with a slider bar underneath to make the similarity rating.
There were three conditions in the similarity matching task: (1) a congruent-congruent (CC) condition, in which photos were matched on model similarity and expression similarity, (2) a congruent-incongruent (CI) condition, in which photos were matched on model similarity but not expression similarity, and (3) an incongruent-congruent (IC) condition, in which photos were not matched on model similarity but were matched on expression similarity.

Methods

Pre-registration
This project was preregistered on the Open Science Framework before any data were collected (OSF DOI: 10.17605/OSF.IO/76Q8W).
The preregistration document specifies the study design, sampling plan, variables, and analysis plan.

Participants
A separate sample of ninety-one participants were recruited from Ontario Tech University's undergraduate student body through SONA.
Four participants were removed due to equipment recording issues.
Participants were then screened on inclusion criteria for a) task attention, and b) fNIRS signal quality.
For attention, participants were required to achieve >= 60% accuracy on the behavioral memory task (chance accuracy = 50%) to ensure sufficient engagement.
One participant failed to meet this criterion.
The remaining 87 participants (69 females and 18 males, M = 21.09, SD = 5.91, range = 17 to 51) were analyzed in the behavioral memory task.
For neural analyses, signal quality of remaining 87 participants was assessed by computing the Peak Spectral Power (PSP) and the Scalp Coupling Index (SCI).
Measures were calculated using a 5-second sliding window across all channels.
Thirty-five participants failed to meet these criteria, and were removed prior to data analysis.
The final sample consisted of 52 participants (39 females and 13 males, M = 21.62, SD = 6.67, range = 17 to 51).

Sample size and power analysis
We conducted a power analysis for a linear mixed model with the simr package.
Our LMM estimated the oxygenated HbO signal as a function of Face-type and Emotion (main effects) with by-Participant random intercept.
Our goal was to obtain 0.8 power [95% CI] at the standard 0.05 alpha error probability.
Fixed effect estimates for Emotion were drawn from Gao et al. (2019).

Stimuli
We used the same stimulus set as in the pilot study.

Apparatus
Participants were tested individually in a quiet dedicated testing room.
fNIRS data was collected using two NIRSport2 systems (NIRx Medical Technologies, Berlin, Germany).
Each NIRSport2 system was equipped with 16 source and 16 detector optodes, and daisy-chained together for a high density 32x32 optode configuration.
Each neighboring pair of source and detector optode is referred to as a channel, resulting in a total of 103 HbO + 103 HbR channels (plus 16 short distance channels).
The average distance between source and detector optodes was 30 mm, and 7 mm for short distance channels, which were placed on a flexible fNIRS head cap (NIRScap) 58 cm in circumference.
The optodes were arranged in a high density 32x32 montage with one bundle of short distance channels.
Light was emitted at 760 nm and 850 nm wavelengths, and the sampling rate was approximately 6.105 Hz.

Design
A full-factorial Face-type (2 levels: Real, Virtual) x Emotion (7 levels: Anger, Disgust, Fear, Joy, Sadness, Surprise, Neutral) x Model (4) x Sex (2 levels: Male, Female) x Repetition (4) experimental design was used, with each participant presented with 448 images.
Stimuli were blocked and counterbalanced by Face-type and Emotion.
Within each of the 56 Face-type-Emotion blocks, participants were presented with 8 distinct model faces (4 male, 4 female).

The experiment began with instructions presented on screen.
The trial timeline consisted of three main epochs: fixation cross, block presentation, and participant feedback.
Each block began with a fixation cross presented for 16 seconds, followed by 8 facial images.
Every face in a block displayed the same emotional expression: anger, disgust, fear, joy, sadness, surprise, or neutral.
Facial images were each presented for 1.5 seconds, with a 250–750 ms (M = 500 ms) interstimulus interval (ISI) between each face.
Participants viewed 56 blocks of 8 faces, each block being either all real or all virtual faces.
To maintain participant attention, participants completed a memory task after each block.
In the task, participants were presented with a model image with the same emotional expression as the rest of the block's images, and asked if the model was shown in the preceding block of 8 faces, with feedback provided using the keyboard (y/n).
The probe face had a 50% chance of either being in the previous block or not.
The experiment continued after seven seconds if no feedback was provided.
Participants were given a break every seven blocks, and prompted to press the space bar when they were ready to continue the experiment.
After the experiment was completed, the experimenter(s) entered the room, removed the fNIRS cap, and the participant was debriefed about the experiment.
Participation in the experiment took approximately 35 minutes.

Analyses
All fNIRS data was preprocessed and analyzed with Python 3.11.9 using MNE (version 1.9.0) and MNE-NIRS (version 0.7.1), which used the Nilearn package (version 0.9.2).
Data were analyzed with a General Linear Model (GLM), followed by a functional connectivity analysis.
The memory task was analyzed in Python using the statsmodels package (version 0.14.4).
The data generated during this study has been made publicly available on the Open Science Framework (OSF).

Activation magnitude with General Linear Model (GLM)
The General Linear Model posits that the observed haemodynamic signal at each channel or Region of Interest (ROI) is a linear combination of task-related regressors convolved with a Hemodynamic Response Function (HRF), plus nuisance regressors (e.g., drift) and residual noise.
A two-way repeated measures GLM was conducted on participants' HbT responses by Face-type (Real, Virtual), Emotion (Anger, Disgust, Fear, Joy, Sadness, Surprise, Neutral) and their interaction.
Pairwise contrasts were then computed between conditions to identify effects of interest.
Since numerous statistical tests are performed across channels and contrasts, p-values were FDR-corrected using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure with a family-wise error rate of alpha = 0.05.

Network mapping with Functional Connectivity Analysis
To characterize the temporal coordination between fNIRS channels during face and emotion processing, functional connectivity matrices were computed using a continuous wavelet transform (CWT)-based spectral connectivity approach.
The morlet wavelet was used because it is suited to capture both slow neural rhythms and faster systemic fluctuations in fNIRS data.
The frequency range was narrowed to five evenly spaced frequencies between 0.2–0.5 Hz due to short epoch length; this range still targets systemic and neurogenic oscillations while avoiding high-frequency noise.
Averaging across these closely spaced frequencies reduces data dimensionality.

Paired Sample t-tests
For each mode (Face type/Emotion), and pair of conditions (e.g., Joy vs. Fear), individual-level connectivity matrices were extracted by averaging across epochs to obtain symmetric channel-by-channel coherence matrices.
Fisher's r-to-z transform (atanh) was applied to each matrix element to normalize the data prior to parametric testing.
Paired t-tests for each unique channel pair (i > j) were then conducted across participants using SciPy's ttest_rel.
p-values were FDR-corrected using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure with a family-wise error rate of alpha = 0.05.

Results

Responses to real and virtual faces
A main effect of Face type was reported for the GLM, with pairwise contrasts revealing greater activation for virtual faces compared to real faces.
The contrast comparing functional connectivity profiles in response to real versus virtual faces revealed significant differences in connectivity across the brain.
Processing real faces was associated with stronger connectivity predominantly between the left to right parietal, left frontal to left parietal, left central/temporal to left parietal, left central/temporal to right parietal, and the right central/temporal to left parietal regions, whereas processing virtual faces was associated with only slightly stronger connectivity between the left central/temporal to right frontal region.

Connectivity profiles of facial emotion
To explore higher level patterns in Emotion, we calculated the ratio of significant channels, where the t-value was positive (red) or negative (blue) for each emotion pair.
We found a cluster of emotions including Anger, Fear, and Joy, that reported stronger connectivity compared to the other emotions.
Of these three, Fear > Anger > Joy, where the stronger functional connectivity for fearful and angry expressions suggests a neural prioritization of faces signalling potential threat, consistent with rapid threat-detection mechanisms.
The other emotions (Neutral, Sadness, Surprise, Disgust) had lower connectivity compared to Anger, Fear, and Joy, with Disgust having higher connectivity than Neutral.

Next we explored higher-order patterns in brain regions connectivity.
For this analysis, we generated a count of the number of statistically significant channels per region that differed across emotion, and plotted as a heatmap.
The left central/temporal, parietal, and right central/temporal regions produced the greatest number of significant connections, suggesting that these regions are more variable in their connectivity patterns across emotions.
In contrast, left and right occipital regions produced the fewest number of significant connections, suggesting that the connectivity within and between these regions are similar in response to all emotions, and that neural indices that differentiate emotion processing likely occur in brain regions outside of the visual cortex.

Interaction of face type and emotion
To further explore the interaction of Face Type and Emotion, we calculated the total number of significant channels for each emotion, separately for real and virtual faces.
Joy, Sadness, and Surprise all had a greater number of significant channels for real faces compared to virtual faces, while Fear, Anger, Disgust, and Neutral all had a greater number of significant channels for virtual faces compared to real faces.

To find which brain regions were driving these differences, we calculated the number of significant channels by face type and region, across all emotions.
The occipital areas were found to have a similar number of significant channels for both face types, while the central/temporal and parietal regions had a greater number of significant channels for real faces compared to virtual faces.
The frontal regions had a greater number of significant channels for virtual faces compared to real faces.
